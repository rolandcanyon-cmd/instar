# The duplicate-session fix, in plain English

## What went wrong

On July 10, your laptop — the machine that owns two of your conversations — crash-looped for about 35 minutes. While it was down, your messages for those conversations arrived at the Mac Mini (the current "front door"). The Mini's routing brain got the answer RIGHT: "these conversations belong to the laptop; it's temporarily dark; queue the messages." Six milliseconds later, an older reflex in the message-handling code ignored that ruling and started its own local copies anyway. When the laptop came back with its original copies still running, you had two live copies of each conversation — double work, crossed wires. And nothing could clean them up: every safety guard that normally prevents killing a session ("it has an open promise to the user", "I'm not the boss machine") was individually correct but jointly made the duplicates immortal. It took a manual cleanup. The same thing had already happened on July 5.

**The root cause in one line: the system computed the right answer and then didn't enforce it.** The routing verdict was advice, and the code that actually creates sessions never asked for it.

## What we're building

**1. Make the verdict binding (prevention).** One checkpoint — `SpawnAdmission` — that every session-creating code path must pass through. If this machine owns the conversation: proceed. If another machine owns it and is alive: forward. If the owner is temporarily dark: NEVER start a bootleg copy — instead hold briefly, take over properly if the owner is provably dead, or save the message and tell you honestly ("that machine is restarting — your message is saved" — or, until the durable queue is live, "please resend in a few minutes"). You are never met with silence for more than 10 minutes, and never lied to about what happened to your message. If the ownership records themselves are broken, the system fails toward answering you (a duplicate is better than silence), but boundedly and loudly — and a circuit breaker stops it repeating.

**2. Clean up automatically when duplicates slip through anyway (healing).** A reconciler runs on the lead machine, spots duplicates, figures out the rightful owner from real evidence (a deliberate pin, the strongest ownership record, a registered live work run — never "who got the last message", because during this bug the WRONG copy gets the last message), repairs the ownership records everywhere, verifies the other machine actually SAW the repair (the incident's own replication channel is not blindly trusted), and then lets the existing, already-safe cleanup close the spare copy. Before anything closes, the system re-checks AT CLOSING TIME that the surviving copy is genuinely alive — so cleanup can never leave you with zero copies. Open promises (commitments) the spare copy was carrying are handed to the surviving copy through an acknowledged transfer — a promise to you is never silently dropped. If both copies are doing real work, nothing is auto-closed; it's escalated to you instead.

**3. Give judgment calls to an AI arbiter — but only inside hard rails (the new pattern).** Two decisions in this flow are genuinely fuzzy: "how long should we wait for a wobbly machine before telling the user?" and "which duplicate deserves to survive when the evidence is messy?" For those, a model gets to choose — but ONLY among options the deterministic code has already proven safe, with a conservative default if the model is unavailable, slow, or answers nonsense. The model can never authorize a takeover (that needs mechanical proof of death), can never move ownership on its own say-so (that needs mechanical work evidence that PRE-DATES the duplicate — so a runaway copy can't manufacture its own alibi), and every single call is logged in full — what it saw, what it chose, and what actually happened — then graded later against reality. If the grading shows the model doesn't beat the simple static rule, the model gets removed. The whole thing ships in stages: observe-only first, deterministic-rules-only next, model-in-shadow after that, and model-acting only after the shadow data proves it's better.

## The three new standards you'd be ratifying (each is its own checkbox)

1. **Judgment Within Floors** — when a decision point has competing signals, you may delegate it to an AI arbiter only inside a deterministic floor: complete safe action space, conservative default, irreversible choices need mechanical corroboration, and the fallback ladder always ends in plain code. Invariants are never delegated.
2. **Decision Provenance & Outcome Review** — every AI judgment call must durably log the full context it was handed and the decision it made, get annotated with what actually happened, and be periodically graded against outcomes. An unlogged judgment call is an unaccountable one.
3. **Ownership-Gated Side Effects** — on a multi-machine setup, anything that creates or revives a session must prove current conversation ownership at the moment it fires. Routing verdicts are binding, not advisory.

## What the review process changed (honesty section)

This spec went through five review rounds — six internal reviewers plus GPT-5.5 and Gemini outside reviews on every changed version. Material findings went 30 → 20 → 5 → 1 → (final verification). The reviewers killed real flaws: the first draft's cleanup would have reproduced the exact July-10 outcome (the "open promise" guard would still have blocked it); the promise-handoff mechanism referenced a database field that doesn't exist and was redesigned from scratch; the healer originally trusted the same replication channel whose failure caused the incident; a "file-viewer denylist" the privacy design leaned on turned out not to exist and is now a named new build; and the outside models pushed us to state plainly what this design does NOT guarantee (no perfect single-owner during network partitions — bounded, loud, self-healing divergence instead) and to make the promise-handoff its own independently-reversible rollout stage.

Two outside-reviewer dissents are recorded rather than adopted: they'd have preferred deferring the Judgment-Within-Floors ratification until shadow data exists (your directive was to ratify in this cycle; the arbiters themselves DO wait for shadow data before acting), and one suggested replacing the survivor-arbiter with a coin-flip-style tie-break (we kept escalate-to-human as the default instead — a coin flip picks a winner with zero evidence).

## What changes for you day-to-day

Nothing until the staged rollout reaches each stage, and the visible changes are all improvements: duplicate sessions stop happening (and self-heal if they do), a dark machine gets you an honest notice instead of silence or a context-less answer from the wrong machine, and promises survive cleanup. Everything ships dark or dry-run first, on the dev machines before the fleet, with every stage independently reversible.

## Build note (Increment 1, 2026-07-11)

Increment 1 is built exactly as approved, with one editorial addition to the spec: an appendix restating the §3.8 self-healing table as machine-checkable declaration blocks (so a lint verifies the retry caps, breakers, and audit locations instead of anyone re-reading prose — no semantic change to the converged text). Two things surfaced during the build's independent second-pass review and were fixed in the code, not waved through: the observe-stage was missing its soak-data hookup (the honest-notice layer would have logged nothing during the observation period, leaving the eventual enforcement flip with no evidence to stand on), and the healer's ownership repair wasn't riding the replication journal (the other machine would never have seen the fix). Both are the kind of gap the observation stage exists to catch before anything enforces.
